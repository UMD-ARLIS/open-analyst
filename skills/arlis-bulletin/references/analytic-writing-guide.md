## 1. Key Intelligence Questions (KIQs)

The KIQ is the central question that every analytic product is built around.

### Requirements
- Open-ended (never yes/no)
- Two parts:
  - Part 1: "What" is happening or "why" is it happening
  - Part 2: "So what" — why does it matter for the customer, company, industry, or national security
- Allows for testable hypotheses or new answers
- Clearly tied to the customer or their priorities

### What a Good KIQ Does
- Scopes the story: if content doesn't tie back to the KIQ, it should be cut
- Provides a roadmap for structuring the product
- Connects to the reader's priorities

### KIQ Examples

**Bad**: "Why did Russia invade Ukraine?"
- Only covers the "what/why" — not forward-looking

**Bad**: "What are the implications of Russia's invasion of Ukraine?"
- Too broad, only covers the second half

**Mediocre**: "Why did Russia invade Ukraine, and to what extent has this move strengthened NATO?"
- Contains an assumption (that it HAS strengthened NATO)

**Good**: "Why did Russia invade Ukraine, and what are the implications for the NATO Alliance?"
- Two-part, covers "what happened" and "why it matters"

### Tips
- If you can't find a good "so what," do more research or brainstorm with colleagues
- Don't be afraid to change your KIQ as you learn more
- If the topic isn't relevant to the reader, consider cutting your losses

## 2. Bottom Line Up Front (BLUF)

The BLUF is the first sentence of the product. It directly answers the KIQ.

### Requirements
- Exactly one sentence
- Directly answers the KIQ
- Uses probabilistic language (never a statement of fact)
- Should be bold and italic in the ARLIS template

### Characteristics of a Well-Written BLUF
- **Precise**: Exact language, well-defined judgment
- **Clear**: Straightforward, reader immediately understands
- **Accurate**: Matches the supporting evidence
- **Consistent**: Addresses the issue the same way as supporting info
- **Complete**: Covers all main analytic issues in the paragraph
- **Digestible**: Reader assimilates the key point without notes

### Common BLUF Problems
If you can't write it in one sentence, you likely have:
- An unclear KIQ
- Too many stories (stick to ONE per product)
- Insufficient research to know your key point

### BLUF Quality Spectrum

**Clear message**: "The plans to the Death Star obtained by Princess Leia show it has a fatal flaw, which a massed X-Wing assault would probably be well-positioned to exploit."
- Reader knows what they need; wants to read more

**Muddled message**: Key takeaway buried in excessive detail
- Reader loses the point

**Message omitted**: "Princess Leia has sent us the plans to the Death Star, which will help our military planning."
- Boring, doesn't convey the key point

**No analytic message**: "The Death Star is a space station the size of a small moon..."
- Descriptive statement of fact, no analysis

### Mini-BLUFs
Each subsequent section should start with an analytic topic sentence (mini-BLUF) that:
- Makes an assessment with probabilistic language
- Helps the reader follow the line of logic
- Is supported by the bullets underneath it

## 3. Outlines and Structure

### The Analytic Story Arc

Products follow this structure:

1. **What?** — What is happening? Who are the key players?
2. **Why?** — Why is it happening?
3. **Impact?** — What is the potential impact? What effect on the customer/industry/sector?
4. **Outlook** — What is likely to happen next?
5. **Implications/Opportunities** — What are the implications? Any risks to mitigate? Actions to consider?

The first portion (What/Why) is mostly backward-looking.
The second portion (Impact/Outlook/Implications) is forward-looking.

For ARLIS Bulletins specifically, this is compressed into two paragraphs:
- **Paragraph 1 ("What")**: Covers the What, Why, and Impact
- **Paragraph 2 ("So What")**: Covers the Outlook, Implications, and Opportunities

### Title Writing

Titles should convey the main point, not just describe the topic.

**Rules**:
- Include the key actor or trend
- Use an active verb
- Convey the "so what" — move beyond description
- If you can't condense your message into a concise title, you lack a clear message

**Bad → Good examples**:
- "The coronavirus variant in California..." → "California COVID-19 Strain Is More Infectious Than the First"
- "Health-care jobs will be able to keep..." → "Health-Care Hiring Is Recession-Proof"
- "Assessing the political strengths and weaknesses of Vladimir Putin" → "Putin Struggling to Assert Power"

## 4. Argumentation

### The Inverted Pyramid
Structure arguments with the most important information first:
1. **Most newsworthy info**: Who? What? When? Where? Why? How?
2. **Important details**: Supporting evidence
3. **Critical background**: Context and sourcing

### Facts, Assessments, and Assumptions
Products must clearly distinguish between:
- **Facts**: Verified information (e.g., "The plans show an exhaust port vulnerability")
- **Assessments**: Analytic judgments using probabilistic language (e.g., "This would almost certainly require X-wing fighters")
- **Assumptions**: Underlying beliefs about reliability or conditions (e.g., "We are confident the plans are legitimate because...")

### Parallel Construction
The structure of the BLUF should mirror the structure of the supporting bullets:
- First clause of the BLUF → first supporting bullet
- Second clause of the BLUF → second supporting bullet

This makes the logic easy to follow and demonstrates evidence backing each claim.

## 5. Probabilistic Language

Analytic products never state predictions as absolutes.

### Confidence Spectrum (low → high)
- Very unlikely
- Unlikely
- Roughly even chance
- Likely
- Very likely
- Almost certainly

### Language Rules
- **Don't say "will"** without a qualifier → Use: may, might, can, could, would likely, will probably, will almost certainly
- **Don't say "should"** without hedging → Use: might, could, may need to, could benefit from, should consider/examine/weigh
- **Never use absolutes** or percentages
- **Never tell stakeholders what they "should" do** — present options and likelihood

### Confidence Examples
- **Low confidence**: "Company X could expand into adjacencies to seek new revenue streams"
- **Medium confidence**: "Company X may need to expand into adjacencies, where competitors have found new revenue streams"
- **High confidence**: "Company X should consider expanding into adjacencies, which would almost certainly lead to new revenue streams"

## 6. The Four Sweeps Checklist

Use this checklist to self-review every bulletin before finalizing.

### Sweep 1: Analytic Message / Clarity
- [ ] Does the product have one analytic message and one principal focus?
- [ ] Does the product answer a clearly defined KIQ?
- [ ] Is the message relevant to our readers?
- [ ] Does the product have a clear BLUF?
- [ ] Does each section tie back to and support the BLUF?
- [ ] Are headings/subheadings in a logical order supporting the argument?

### Sweep 2: Structure / Argumentation
- [ ] Do topic sentences follow the analytic story arc?
- [ ] Could a reader follow the analysis by scanning only headlines and topic sentences?
- [ ] Does the structure follow a clear framework (What/So What/Now What)?
- [ ] Is the argument convincing and easy to follow?
- [ ] Are topic sentences supported by strong evidence?
- [ ] Does the product distinguish between facts, assessments, and assumptions?

### Sweep 3: Prose / Writing
- [ ] Are paragraphs and sentences short?
- [ ] Is active voice used?
- [ ] Grammar, punctuation, and parallel construction checked?
- [ ] Is jargon minimized, and key terms defined?
- [ ] Is language simple and direct (no flowery words)?

### Sweep 4: Graphics / Formatting
- [ ] Do graphics (if any) support the argument and add value?
- [ ] Are graphics easy to read/understand?
- [ ] Are titles, captions, and labels clear and sourced?
- [ ] Is everything sourced properly (endnotes in Chicago style)?
- [ ] Are headers/footers formatted correctly (UNCLASSIFIED / NOT FOR PUBLIC RELEASE)?
- [ ] Are classification markings present on every page?

## 7. Analytic Writer's Master Checklist

Does the report, assessment, or bulletin:

1. Answer the sponsor's key questions?
2. Include both the "what" and the "so what"?
3. Present clearly and accurately all forces and dynamics at play?
4. Articulate a clear line of analysis, putting the bottom line up front?
5. State the main point in the title and first paragraph?
6. Ensure each section, paragraph, and sentence advances the storyline?
7. Provide sufficient reasoning and compelling evidence for all judgments?
8. Distinguish between analytic judgments and factual information?
9. Highlight contrary information inconsistent with the line of analysis?
10. Note the impact of information gaps on the analysis?
11. Reflect alternative views or explanations where appropriate?
12. Clearly express levels of confidence and reasons for uncertainty?
13. Avoid bias, advocacy, or value-laden terms?
14. Incorporate graphics (if applicable) to advance the message?
